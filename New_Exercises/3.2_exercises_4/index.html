Exercise  1： Let BCEA be a parallelogram. C, D, B are collinear and CD=2DB. EB=3DA. Prove that CA⊥AB.
\(\because \) C, D, B are collinear and CD=2DB  \(\therefore \small\overrightarrow{AD}=\dfrac{2 \small\overrightarrow{AB}}{3} + \dfrac{\small\overrightarrow{AC}}{3}\).
\(\because \) BCEA is a parallelogram  \(\therefore \small\overrightarrow{AE}=- \small\overrightarrow{AB} + \small\overrightarrow{AC}\).
\(\because \) EB=3DA  \( \therefore9 \small\overrightarrow{AD}^{2} - \small\overrightarrow{EB}^{2}=9 \small\overrightarrow{AD}^{2} - \left(\small\overrightarrow{AB} - \small\overrightarrow{AE}\right)^{2}=9 \left(\dfrac{2 \small\overrightarrow{AB}}{3} + \dfrac{\small\overrightarrow{AC}}{3}\right)^{2} - \left(2 \small\overrightarrow{AB} - \small\overrightarrow{AC}\right)^{2}=8 \small\overrightarrow{AB} \cdot \small\overrightarrow{AC}=0.\)
In conclusion,  \(\small\overrightarrow{AB} \cdot \small\overrightarrow{AC}=0\), that is, CA⊥AB.
Exercise  3： Let C, D, B be collinear and CD=2DB. B is the midpoint of EA. EC=3DA. Prove that CA⊥AB.
\(\because \) C, D, B are collinear and CD=2DB  \(\therefore \small\overrightarrow{AD}=\dfrac{2 \small\overrightarrow{AB}}{3} + \dfrac{\small\overrightarrow{AC}}{3}\).
\(\because \) B is the midpoint of EA  \(\therefore \small\overrightarrow{AE}=2 \small\overrightarrow{AB}\).
\(\because \) EC=3DA  \( \therefore9 \small\overrightarrow{AD}^{2} - \small\overrightarrow{CE}^{2}=9 \small\overrightarrow{AD}^{2} - \left(- \small\overrightarrow{AC} + \small\overrightarrow{AE}\right)^{2}=9 \left(\dfrac{2 \small\overrightarrow{AB}}{3} + \dfrac{\small\overrightarrow{AC}}{3}\right)^{2} - \left(2 \small\overrightarrow{AB} - \small\overrightarrow{AC}\right)^{2}=8 \small\overrightarrow{AB} \cdot \small\overrightarrow{AC}=0.\)
In conclusion,  \(\small\overrightarrow{AB} \cdot \small\overrightarrow{AC}=0\), that is, CA⊥AB.
